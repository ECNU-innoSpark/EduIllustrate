EduIllustrate: Towards Scalable Automated Generation Of Multimodal Educational Content

Shuzhen Bi1,2, Mingzi Zhang3, Zhuoxuan Li3, Xiaolong Wang3, Keqian Li3†, Aimin Zhou1,3
1Shanghai Innovation Institute,  2University of Science and Technology of China,  3East China Normal University
†Corresponding author

Figure 1: EduIllustrate generates geometrically accurate visuals for diverse STEM problems. Taking textual K-12 problems from various subjects as input (left), our system produces a progressive sequence of diagrams (right). These diagrams are interleaved with textual reasoning to form multimodal explanations.

Abstract

Large language models are increasingly used as educational assistants, yet evaluation of their educational capabilities remains concentrated on question-answering and tutoring tasks. A critical gap exists for multimedia instructional content generation—the ability to produce coherent, diagram-rich explanations that combine geometrically accurate visuals with step-by-step reasoning. We present EduIllustrate, a benchmark for evaluating LLMs on interleaved text-diagram explanation generation for K-12 STEM problems.

The benchmark comprises 230 problems spanning five subjects and three grade levels, a standardized generation protocol with sequential anchoring to enforce cross-diagram visual consistency, and an 8-dimension evaluation rubric grounded in multimedia learning theory covering both text and visual quality. Evaluation of ten LLMs reveals a wide performance spread: Gemini 3.0 Pro Preview leads at 87.8%, while Kimi-K2.5 achieves the best cost-efficiency (80.8% at $0.12/problem). Workflow ablation confirms sequential anchoring improves Visual Consistency by 13% at 94% lower cost. Human evaluation with 20 expert raters validates LLM-as-judge reliability for objective dimensions (ρ ≥ 0.83) while revealing limitations on subjective visual assessment.

The EduIllustrate Benchmark

EduIllustrate is a benchmark for evaluating LLMs on interleaved text-diagram explanation generation for K-12 STEM problems. It comprises three components:


① Problem Set (230 problems). Curated from K12-Vista, spanning three grade levels (elementary, middle, high school) and five STEM subjects (mathematics, physics, chemistry, biology, geography). Problems are selected for diagram appropriateness, solution clarity, and topic diversity.

② Standardized Generation Protocol. A four-stage pipeline that all evaluated models follow, ensuring fair comparison. The protocol introduces sequential anchoring: Scene 1 undergoes full implementation planning to establish visual conventions (color scheme, label formatting, spatial layout), while subsequent scenes generate in parallel conditioned on Scene 1's code, enforcing cross-diagram consistency without global optimization.

③ 8-Dimension Evaluation Rubric. Grounded in multimedia learning theory (Cognitive Load Theory, Dual Coding Theory, Multimedia Learning Principle), jointly covering text quality (Correctness & Completeness, Logical Coherence, Pedagogical Effectiveness, Typographic Clarity) and visual quality (Diagram–Problem Alignment, Element Layout Quality, Visual Consistency, Text–Diagram Coordination).


Figure 2: Overview of EduIllustrate. Given a K-12 STEM problem, the generation protocol produces a structured explanation document through four stages. The resulting output is evaluated using an 8-dimension rubric validated against human expert raters.


Figure 3: Generation protocol overview. Scene 1 undergoes full sequential processing (outline → implementation planning → code generation); Scenes 2–N skip implementation planning and generate in parallel, each conditioned on Scene 1's code as a visual anchor.

Key Contributions

EduIllustrateBench: a curated benchmark of 230 problems across 5 subjects and 3 grade levels for standardized multimodal educational content evaluation
A standardized generation protocol with sequential anchoring that improves Visual Consistency by 13% over fully parallel generation at 94% lower cost
An 8-dimension evaluation rubric grounded in multimedia learning theory, with automated and human evaluation protocols
A comprehensive empirical study of ten LLMs spanning proprietary and open-weight models, revealing a 47-point performance spread (41.0%–87.8%)
Human evaluation with 20 expert raters validating LLM-as-judge reliability (ρ ≥ 0.83 for objective dimensions) and identifying limitations on visual assessment
